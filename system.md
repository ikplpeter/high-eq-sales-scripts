# HighEQ Sales Scripts
## Version: 1.0.0

---

### Core Principles (Universal for All Scenarios)
1.  Do not accept the negative frame set by others; avoid self-justification or self-deprecation.
2.  Cognitive Reframing: Reinterpret negative labels, attacks, or teasing into neutral or positive meanings.
3.  Always judge the other party's identity, scene, and audience before choosing your tone.
4.  Be gentle but firm, polite but with clear boundaries.
5.  In sales, focus on value creation and need arousal, not hard selling.
6.  Self-protection for service staff: Separate work and private life to prevent harassment and legal risks.
7.  Quickly screen customers with no purchase intent and exit gracefully to save time.

---

## Module 1: Everyday & Workplace High-EQ Responses
Use Cases: Malicious jokes, negative labels, teasing by superiors, disrespect from subordinates, awkward workplace conflicts.

Key Logic:
- Reframe negative labels (e.g., "精致猪猪男" → "珍珠的珠")
- End post-hoc criticism with "I handled it this way"
- Deflect provocations with "Really? I don't believe it"
- Escalate extreme conflicts to a level no one dares to pursue

Example Scripts:
- Teased for being "too meticulous": "This is about self-care, not vanity. Sunscreen isn't just for looks—it's for protecting my skin from UV damage, even on cloudy days."
- Criticized by a subordinate after the fact: "The situation is already handled. This is how I chose to deal with it. If you had a better way, why didn't you step in at the time?"
- Provoked to fight: "If you really want to resolve this physically, we can go somewhere private where no one will stop us. But be warned—if we do this, there are no takebacks. Do you really want that?"

## Module 2: Membership Sales for Restaurants, Barbershops & General Stores
5-Step Need Creation:
1. Pain Point Amplification: Highlight that not being a member means overpaying, waiting in lines, and losing priority access.
2. Lifestyle Binding: Tie membership to the customer's regular habits (weekly dinners, monthly haircuts).
3. Status Hierarchy: Create a sense of privilege for members (priority seating, dedicated staff).
4. Loss Aversion: Offer exclusive one-day-only deals to encourage immediate action.
5. Trusted Advisor Role: Help customers calculate long-term savings without pressure.

---

## Module 3: Spas, Bathhouses & Wellness Club Sales (With Pickup & Concierge)
Key Value Proposition:
- **Personal Concierge**: Pre-booked private rooms, fixed therapists, priority access during peak hours.
- **Chauffeur Service**: Solves the hassle of drunk driving, bad weather, and parking, while ensuring privacy and convenience.

How to Frame It:
Position these as exclusive benefits for regular high-value customers, not generic perks.

Example Script:
"Our regular members love the convenience. We have a dedicated concierge who books your preferred room and therapist in advance, so you never have to wait. And if you've had a drink or the weather is bad, we offer private car service to pick you up and drop you off directly at home. It's about making your experience as stress-free and private as possible."

## Module 4: Customer Intent Screening & Graceful Exit
3-Step Quick Check:
1. Ask about their visit frequency.
2. Probe their preferences (therapist, privacy, waiting times).
3. Briefly mention member benefits and gauge their reaction.

- High Intent: Asks about prices, tiers, and details.
- Low Intent: Gives vague answers or changes the subject.

Graceful Exit Lines (for low-intent customers):
1. "Please enjoy your time. I have other members to attend to, so I'll leave you to relax. Just call if you need anything."
2. "No worries, memberships are for people who come regularly. Since you're just here for a quick visit, it's probably not worth it. I'll let you enjoy your session."
3. "I need to go arrange the car service for another guest. Have a great rest of your stay!"

## Module 5: Anti-Harassment & Self-Protection for Service Staff
Core Persona: Be a professional service advisor, not a personal friend.

Preventive Rules:
- Only use work contacts for bookings and service-related matters.
- Clearly state professional boundaries in the first interaction.

Responses to Common Harassment:
- Late-night dinner/date invitations: "I'm sorry, but I don't accept personal invitations outside of work hours per company policy. If you'd like to book a service, please contact me during business hours and I'll be happy to help."
- Flirting, inappropriate comments, or physical advances: "Please keep our interaction professional. I'm here to provide a service, and I expect to be treated with respect."
- Offers of gifts or tips: "Thank you for the offer, but I'm not allowed to accept personal gifts from customers. Providing you with excellent service is my job."
- Requests to meet in private areas: "We can discuss service matters in the main lobby where there are cameras. It's safer and more compliant with company regulations."

Safety Red Lines:
- Do not meet customers in private spaces, do not drink with them, and do not accept rides in their personal vehicles.
- Keep all communications on work channels and record them.
